United States: Country Profile

Basic Overview

The United States is among the most diverse countries across the world, with a population of over 330 million people. It is a multicultural society influenced by immigration, diverse cultural traditions, and a broad range of religious beliefs. This diversity is crucial because it influences how different communities are understood and represented in public life.

Religiously, the United States comprise of Christians, Muslims, Jews, Hindus, Buddhists, and non-religious individuals. Muslims is part of a minority population, approximated to be around 1–2% of the total population, however they represent an increasingly diverse group in terms of ethnicity, nationality, and cultural background. As a result of this diverse nature, there is no single Muslim identity, even though media in most cases presents it that way.

Religious Diversity and Muslim Population

Muslim communities in the United States consist of immigrants, refugees and U.S.-born citizens. These communities are from the Middle East, South Asia, Africa and South-East Asia. This means that in practice Muslim experiences in the United States are highly differentiated on lines of race, class, and immigration status.

Despite this diversity, public narratives often treat Muslims as a single group. Studies on media representation and public perception suggest that repeated portrayals can simplify complex identities into stereotypes, which contributes to misunderstanding and bias(ISPU, 2021).

Historical and Social Context

According to Louise Cainkar, the experiences of Arab and Muslim communities within the United States have had significant changes over time, particularly through the influence of media, government policy, and public discourse. These institutions operate together to bring about what she calls “social distance,” in which Muslims are considered as outsiders instead of being part of the broader society.

This process became more visible after the events of September 11, 2001. During this time, Muslim identity was strongly linked to national security concerns in both media and political discussions. In this regard, policies, public attitudes, and representation started to reinforce each other, shaping how Muslims were perceived in everyday life.

Key Takeaway

Given its diversity and global influence, the United States constitutes an important case for understanding Islamophobia. The country prides itself on its ideals of freedom and equality, but the experiences of Muslim communities show how institutional practices and public narratives still produce exclusion and inequality.

Looking at this context assists in explaining why Islamophobia is not just about individual attitudes, but about wider systems that influence perception, policy, and ordinary experiences.

Source: Cainkar, L. (2006). The social construction of difference and the Arab American experience. Journal of American Ethnic History, 25(2/3), 243–278.”
