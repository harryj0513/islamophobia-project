Islamophobia in U.S. Media Representation

Islamophobia in the United States has been shaped through interconnected social, political, and media institutions that construct negative representations of Muslims. These representations intensified after the events of September 11, 2001, when public discourse increasingly associated Islam with violence, terrorism, and national security threats.

Media representation plays a central role in constructing Islamophobia, but it operates alongside broader systems of power. As Louise Cainkar argues, institutions such as media, government, and public discourse work together to produce “social distance,” reinforcing stereotypes, shaping public attitudes, and supporting policies that marginalize Muslim communities.
